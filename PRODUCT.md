# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Compass is for people who want a focused calendar system without the drag of
complicated, slow, bulky, or expensive productivity tools. They plan their day
seriously, prefer fast keyboard-friendly workflows, and want events, tasks, and
future planning to stay organized without turning the tool itself into another
project.

## Product Purpose

Compass helps users plan faster, lock in on what matters now, and keep calendar
work light enough to use every day. It combines day planning, week planning,
tasks, someday items, local-first anonymous use, account-backed storage, and
optional Google Calendar sync into a durable web-first product that stays useful
without adding unnecessary scope.

Success means users can move from month to week to day, capture future work,
adjust schedules, and focus on one task with very little friction.

## Brand Personality

Minimal, focused, durable, and fast. Compass should feel calm and capable rather
than flashy; precise rather than sterile; long-lived rather than trend-chasing.
The voice should be plain, direct, and quietly confident.

## Anti-references

Compass should not feel like a bloated productivity suite, a gamified habit app,
a corporate SaaS dashboard, or a slow calendar clone packed with secondary
features. Avoid decorative complexity, noisy onboarding, marketing-heavy product
surfaces inside the app, and patterns that make the interface feel expensive to
operate mentally or financially.

## Design Principles

- Keep the calendar fast enough that planning never becomes the work.
- Make focus the default: surface the next useful action without surrounding it
  with ceremony.
- Treat keyboard users as first-class users while keeping pointer interaction
  natural and discoverable.
- Preserve local-first trust: users should feel the product works before they
  commit to an account or Google connection.
- Build for years of daily use, with restrained choices that age well.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Compass should meet practical WCAG AA expectations for contrast, keyboard
access, focus visibility, semantic controls, and reduced-motion respect. The
product should be accessible as a baseline of craft, without turning the whole
experience into a compliance-first interface that overwhelms the planning flow.
